Reality is complex. Especially for people like us who look for so many things and relations at the same time. I am not the only one who starts from “oh! This particular panchayat collected 12,72,300 last year” and soon finds himself tangled into questions such as “why do panchayats differ in their tax collection”, “oh! It must be the difference in the tax base”, “wait! But what if panchayats differ in their compliance rate?”, “What about differences in tax administration/enforcement?”, and so on…

  

A simple observation turns into a myriad of questions. It is food for our own curiosity but is bad for research. How often do you find that you started with a naive curiosity—which quite possibly could have been a good research question—and find yourself too overwhelmed to do any meaningful research?

  

There are countless systematic guides on how to do research. However, I didn't find a manual for the perplexed. Someone once said, “the best piece of writing is, writing for yourself. Write something you wished you had access to”.

  

The agenda is clear. How do we move from curiosity to research question? How do we use the web of thoughts to inform our research?

—

  

I take it given that beneath every attempt at research or broadly a 4 am, 43 tab frenzy has a rational basis. There is a great thirst to understand, to explain, and quite possibly create something. This is not a common occurrence. Most people are curious, only a few are courageous enough to seek. Of these, most people take a plunge, only a few know how to swim. The fault isn't in the madness, it's in the method. You were endowed with the potential, now it is your responsibility to sharpen it.

  

Do you remember how you know so much about property tax? It didn't start just yesterday. Even before you came to know about this data source, you knew about the institutional details of Karnataka. And before property tax, there were property rights. And no one can deny it was your curiosity in land, its use, its ownership that propelled your journey. To top it all, in your childhood you saw a big stamp on an A4 paper. It was a land document. Quite possibly it was the genesis of everything. 

  

To an outsider your interests might look chaotic but there has been a sustained, systematic effort to curate your curiosity. It's time we do the same to the method of research. I'll let you out on a secret—its super easy. Well at least the idea part. We will come back to the practicing later.

  

What is the difference between “a curiosity filled question” and “a research question”? In substance? None. The difference lies in the method, in the scope of the question and the claims of the answer. The rest are aesthetics. Another useful distinction (again due to method) is, “research questions” are designed to be answerable. This imagery brings out the difference: Say you want to cross a wheat field (or even a tulip if you fancy) to go to the little house at the end. You could take the shortest way, running and rummaging through the plants, the uneven land, and god knows what's below the colorful scene. Or you could take a seemingly weird and arbitrary snake shaped space of land left in between the field—otherwise known as a road.

  

For a long time, you have heard your imagination say ‘jump, dive, climb,’ but the road quietly whispers, ‘Just walk.’ Yes, it is deceptively simple. I told you.

How to take the road: Or an inquiry into how not to keep running in the field like a confused goose.

  
  
  

I will admit, for long narrowing down the question, making it falsifiable, or broadly making it answerable or simply walking on the road has felt like cheating. A disservice to the web of relations and questions I have created. An act of selling out even. I won't even talk about how arbitrary this whole thing is, in and out. How can I focus on just a part when the whole stretches before me? How—and why—do I even dare answer this sliver of a question when the mammoth of interconnected ideas stands tall?

  

I won't convince you to take the road, if you don't want to. But I will tell you what you miss out if you don't.

—

  

Let's define what a hurdle is when it comes to moving from curiosity to question?

  

Say, you saw an interesting phenomenon, an amusing counterfactual, or simply even wanted to know some facts of the world. [Re: Issiah Berlin on types of questions]. 

  

What is the current population of India? Well that's straight forward. It's straightforward in at least two sense—it can be looked at (you don't have to go and count each and every indian) and it's settled (for others and for you—in itself it doesn't give rise to more questions, it doesn't need explanation. Okay maybe it does. Looking at you, you little rebel. Hold your horses. It's only gonna get nastier from here.)

  

What is the Dependency ratio for India? The count of people below 15 and people above 59 as a share of count of people between 15-59. This is not so simple. Other than the fact that it is made of straight empirical facts (we can get the Nr and Dr from the same source we got the plain population numbers), the ratio requires interpretation and the bar for explanation is much higher. High population numbers can be explained with relative strength of fertility rate and death rate; the net addition to an already existing stock of people but dependency ratio requires much more than that—it needs a theory. A theory of demographic transition. [Tim Dyson, 2008; DE, 1997]

  

I know, you don't struggle with these questions and to be fair this is still answerable, at least in the limits of already existing facts.

  

But what about questions like, “what would have happened or better still what would happen to the dependency ratio if India were to implement a 1 or 2 child policy?” 

  

We can't just calculate it, even with all the available population facts. Yes, we are still going to calculate (estimate) it and available facts are not the only ingredients. How do we do this? When there are so many factors to consider, so many assumptions, and likely many different theories as well. 

  

Welcome to the road away from perplexity! 

  

Unlike working with physical things, research or making ideal objects [Plato, theory of forms; allegory of cave] is reversible. Say you defined an estimator which only takes first observations into consideration and disregards the rest. Of course it's weird, arbitrary, and likely to be filled with issues but there is nothing in the world that stops you from simply tracing your step back and redefining the estimator as a new function that now takes the first two observations into consideration instead of just one.

  

The research process is 100% traversable in either direction —unless you deleted all the raw data just after making up the first estimator and estimates.

  

You can always take one step forward, 2 steps backwards on a road.
